Why Synthetic Programming?

Chris Mueller

This is a work in progress...

CorePy was originally developed to provide a convenient method for developing AlitVec/VMX programms from Python. As CorePy evolved, a new programming paradigm, synthetic programming, was developed to help manage the task of generating hardware instructions at run time.

Synthetic programming is built around three observations. First, only small portions of most applications require truly high-performance implementations. User interfaces and small-scale data processing tasks execute at acceptable speeds in interpreted languages. Occasionally a task will become noticabily slow when executed by a scripting language, at which point the task may be ported to a compiled language. This requires the developer to switch languages and add an additional level of compmlexity to the toolchain.

The next observation is that the common compiled languages, C, C++, and FORTRAN were designed for machine models that are no longer realistic. Attempts to accomodate vector processors and multi-core systems at the language-level have been largely unsucessful, often requiring the users to use intrinsics that are merely thin wrappers around assembly instructions. These languages simply do not have the semantic breadth to handle the demands of modern hardware.

The final observation is that developers are very productive with highly interactive languages. Langauges that allow developers to iterate quickly through a range of solutions keep developers engaged in the development process. Even short delays imparted by a 20 second compilation can be enough to lead the developer to a 2 minute Slashdot break.
